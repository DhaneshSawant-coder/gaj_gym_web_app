01
Muscle Science
Progressive Overload: The Fundamental Law of Muscle Growth
→

Progressive overload is the single most important principle in building muscle. It simply means doing more over time — more weight, more reps, more sets, or less rest. Your muscle fibres only grow when they are forced to adapt to a stress they haven't encountered before.

In practice: if you bench pressed 60 kg for 8 reps last week, aim for 62.5 kg or 9 reps this week. Even the smallest increment counts. Without this progressive challenge, your body has no reason to build new muscle tissue — it has already adapted to the current demand.

Track every session. A training log is not optional if growth is your goal — it is the tool that makes progressive overload systematic rather than accidental.

02
Nutrition
Protein Timing — Does It Actually Matter for Hypertrophy?
→

The short answer: total daily protein intake matters far more than timing. Research consistently shows that hitting your protein target for the day — roughly 1.6 to 2.2 grams per kg of bodyweight — is the primary driver of muscle protein synthesis.

That said, timing is not completely irrelevant. Consuming 20–40 g of protein within 1–2 hours after training can support recovery, particularly if you trained in a fasted state or your last meal was several hours ago. The so-called "anabolic window" is real but much wider than the old 30-minute myth suggested.

Priority order: hit your total daily protein first, spread it across 3–5 meals, and then consider post-workout timing as a secondary refinement — not a replacement for consistent daily intake.

03
Programming
Volume vs Intensity: How to Structure Your Weekly Training Split
→

Volume refers to the total amount of work done (sets × reps × weight). Intensity refers to how heavy the load is relative to your maximum. Both drive muscle growth, but they do so through slightly different mechanisms — volume accumulates muscle damage and metabolic stress, while high intensity recruits the largest, strongest motor units.

For hypertrophy, most research supports 10–20 working sets per muscle group per week, performed in the 6–20 rep range at 60–80% of your one-rep max. For strength, fewer sets at higher intensity (85–95% 1RM) in the 1–5 rep range are more effective.

A practical split for most people: 3–4 days of training, hitting each muscle group twice per week. Push/Pull/Legs or Upper/Lower splits work well. Avoid cramming all your volume into one session — frequency and recovery between sessions matter as much as the session itself.

04
Recovery
Why Sleep Is Your Most Powerful Anabolic Tool
→

You do not grow in the gym. You grow while you sleep. Training is the stimulus — sleep is when your body actually repairs the damaged muscle fibres, builds them back thicker, and releases the majority of its daily growth hormone (GH). Around 70% of daily GH secretion occurs during deep slow-wave sleep.

Sleep deprivation has a devastating effect on muscle gains. Studies show that cutting sleep from 8 hours to 5.5 hours shifts the composition of weight lost from fat to muscle — meaning poor sleep actively causes muscle loss even in a calorie surplus. Testosterone levels also drop significantly after just one week of restricted sleep.

Aim for 7–9 hours per night. Keep a consistent sleep schedule, avoid screens for 30 minutes before bed, and keep your room cool and dark. If you are training hard at Gaj Fitness and eating right but not recovering, poor sleep is almost certainly the missing variable.
